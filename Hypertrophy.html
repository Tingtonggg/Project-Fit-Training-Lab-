MECHANISMS · 01

Definition & Primary Drivers of Muscle Hypertrophy

PRIMARY

Mechanical Tension

Physical stretch force from load activates mTOR signaling pathway. Strongest, most consistent hypertrophy stimulus — the irreplaceable core driver.

SECONDARY

Metabolic Stress

Metabolite accumulation (lactate / phosphate) causes cellular swelling. Primary mechanism in high-volume / low-load training.

DEBATED

Muscle Damage

Micro-tears from eccentric contractions. Recent evidence (2022–2024): not a required stimulus — may be a byproduct, not a primary driver.

Relative Contribution to Hypertrophy

Key Insight: Mechanical tension is the only mechanism with strong, consistent evidence across all rep ranges and loads.

Schoenfeld (2010) J Strength Cond Res · Wackerhage et al. (2019) J Appl Physiol

INTENSITY · 02

Training to Failure & Intensity Science

Core Principle: Proximity to Failure Drives Growth

Only near failure (RIR ≤ 3) can high-threshold motor units be fully recruited — this maximizes mechanical tension, the key hypertrophy stimulus.

Load Independence Principle

High vs low load produces similar hypertrophy — proximity to failure is the key variable. 5–30 RM all effective (Schoenfeld et al., 2017).

Practical RIR Strategy

80%

of sets: RIR 1–3 (leave 1–3 reps in reserve)

20%

of sets: RIR 0 (true failure — use sparingly)

Avoid

chronic failure every set → prevents systemic fatigue

RIR vs. Motor Unit Recruitment

RIR 0 (Failure)RIR 8 (Easy)

Schoenfeld et al. (2017) JSCR · Schoenfeld et al. (2021) J Funct Morphol Kinesiol

RECRUITMENT · 03

Muscle Fiber Recruitment & Load Relationship

Henneman's Size Principle

Activation order: slow-twitch (Type I) first → fast-twitch (Type II) last. Near failure, ANY load can fully recruit all fiber types.

LOAD ZONES & FIBER RECRUITMENT
Light — 30–40% 1RM Effective near failure

More reps needed · Higher metabolic fatigue · Same hypertrophy when taken to failure

Moderate — 50–60% 1RM Best balance

Optimal efficiency-to-fatigue ratio · Practical for most exercises

Heavy — 70–85% 1RM Fastest recruitment

Quickest path to full Type II recruitment · Higher CNS demand

Type II Fiber Recruitment vs. Load + Proximity to Failure

Light + Failure ≈ Heavy + Failure → Equal hypertrophy outcome

Henneman et al. (1965) J Neurophysiol · Mitchell et al. (2012) J Appl Physiol

RECOVERY · 04

Balancing Failure, Fatigue & Recovery

STIMULUS

Proximity to Failure

✓ Provides essential mechanical tension for muscle growth

✓ Must be near failure (RIR ≤ 3) to be effective

✓ Place failure sets at end of session for max effect

FATIGUE

Too Much of a Good Thing

✗ Chronic failure every set → neural fatigue

✗ Systemic fatigue accumulates → compromises long-term progress

✗ High effort, insufficient target muscle stimulus

RECOVERY

Ensure Full Restoration

✓ 7–9 hours of sleep per night

✓ Adequate protein (1.6–2.2 g/kg/day)

✓ Deload week every 4–6 weeks

Periodization: RIR Strategy Over a Training Block

80–90% of sets

Keep at RIR 1–3

10–20% of sets

True failure (RIR 0)

Schoenfeld et al. (2021) J Funct Morphol Kinesiol

STRETCH · 05

Stretch-Mediated Hypertrophy (2023–2025 New Evidence)

KEY FINDING

Training at long muscle lengths triggers a unique growth stimulus — promoting sarcomere addition and regional hypertrophy. Local growth response up to

1.8×*

*Regional data (distal biceps femoris), not whole-muscle average (Maeo et al., 2024)

Recommended Exercises (Stretch Position)

Quads — Leg press (deep) / Nordic curl
Hamstrings — Romanian deadlift (full ROM) / Seated leg curl
Chest — Incline dumbbell fly / Cable fly (long ROM)
Key Tip: Deep ROM is the priority · Pause 0.5–1s at the stretched position · Peak contraction is NOT the priority.

Stretch vs. Shortened Position — Growth Response

Maeo et al. (2024) Med Sci Sports Exerc · Pedrosa et al. (2022) Med Sci Sports Exerc

MECHANICS · 08

Squat & Bench Press Force Path Mechanics

🏋️

Squat — Hip-Dominant Drive

🟠
Hip-Dominant Drive

Actively sit back as you descend, hip flexion leads. Knees track in line with toes — no valgus collapse.

🟠
Maintain Upright Torso

Brace your core, avoid excessive forward lean. Gaze slightly up, maintain neutral spine.

🟠
Heels Stay Grounded

If heels rise, ankle mobility needs work. Use a heel wedge or elevated surface to assist.

⭐
Deep Stretch Bonus

Quads reach maximum stretch at the bottom. Pause 0.5s to amplify the stretch stimulus.

🏋️

Bench Press — Chest-First Drive

🔵
Scapular Retraction & Depression

Actively retract and depress shoulder blades. Protects shoulder joint and maximizes pec activation.

🔵
Chest-First Drive

Imagine pushing both sides of the bar toward each other. Focus on chest contraction, not arm push.

🔵
Forearms Perpendicular to Floor

Adjust grip width so forearms are vertical at the bottom. Optimal force transfer, minimal joint stress.

⭐
Controlled Descent Path

Bar touch point: 2–3cm below nipple line. Follow an arc path on descent (not straight down).

McGill (2007) Low Back Disorders · Schick et al. (2010) J Strength Cond Res

ROOT CAUSE · 09

Why Hard Work Doesn't Always Equal Results

01
Incorrect Force Patterns

Compensator muscles dominate — target muscles underactivated and undertrained.

02
Insufficient Joint Mobility

Can't reach optimal position — stretch stimulus is severely limited.

03
Weak Neural Activation

Poor mind-muscle connection — target muscle can't contract fully even when load is adequate.

04
Inflated Training Volume

Feels exhausting but target muscle lacks sufficient mechanical tension.

05
Poor Movement Quality

Uncontrolled eccentric phase wastes critical growth stimulus.

80%

of trainees have force pathway deviations that silently reduce training effectiveness.

Your effort deserves the right force pathway.

Cook & Voight (1994) JOSPT · Schoenfeld (2010) NSCA

SOLUTION · 10

Functional Training: Correcting Force Pathways

① IMPROVE JOINT MOBILITY
Ankles

Wall ankle stretch · 2×30s each side

Hip Flexors

Kneeling lunge stretch · 2×30s each side

Thoracic Spine

Cat-cow + 4-point rotation

Shoulders

Band external rotation · 3×15 reps

Goal: 3–5× per week · Always mobility before training

② BUILD NEUROMUSCULAR CONNECTION
Squat

Heel-elevated goblet squat (isolate ankle limitation)

Bench Press

Band-around-wrists drill (scapular activation)

Deadlift

Bulgarian split squat (unilateral stability)

Curls

Spider curl (eliminate compensation)

Each: 3×8–10 · Focus entirely on the feel

③ CORRECT FORCE PATHWAYS
Visual Feedback

Mirror or video to verify body position and form

Kinesthetic Cues

Hip hinge feel · Chest-led push sensation

Form-First Rule

Form first, then load — no exceptions.

Assessment Cycle

Film and compare every 4 weeks · Monthly coach check-in

Progressive: form first, then progressively add load

Cook (2010) Movement · Page et al. (2010) Janda Approach

SUMMARY · KEY TAKEAWAYS

The Science of Hypertrophy — 6 Core Principles

01
Mechanical Tension First

The primary hypertrophy driver. Near failure (RIR 1–3) maximizes motor unit recruitment.

02
Length > Shortening

Loaded stretch = 1.8× local growth response. Deep ROM is the key optimization variable.

03
Frequency Matters

2–3× per muscle/week · 48h gap · Frequency split improves results by ~25%.

04
Tempo Control

Eccentric 2–3s = peak growth efficiency · ~1.4× that of a 1-second eccentric.

05
Posture is the Foundation

Wrong force pathway = diminishing returns, no matter how hard you train.

06
Technique is Everything

Squat hip-dominant · Bench with scapular retraction · Make the target muscle actually do the work.
